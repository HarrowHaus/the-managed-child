# VOICE — the prose register, as a spec

*Source of truth for how the prose sounds. `CONVENTIONS.md` governs what may be claimed; this file governs how it is said. Where a draft and this file disagree, this file wins. Derived from the settled register of `essays/the-administrators.md` (2026-07-09).*

---

## The correction this file exists to make

`CONVENTIONS.md` §8 and `DECISIONS.md` S-7 call the essay register the **"inhabited voice."** That phrase has been read — wrongly — as licence for a first-person narrator who addresses the reader, stages his own reasoning, and instructs the reader how to feel. It is not.

**"Inhabited" means the author's *judgment* is present, not the author's *person*.** The voice inhabits the argument: it decides what matters, connects what belongs together, and says plainly what the evidence shows. It does not appear on the page as a personality, and it never tells the reader what to experience.

The register is **intellectual history with a thesis** — the prose of serious writers in this field. State the claim. Lay the evidence. Draw the connection. Stop.

---

## 1. The evidence carries the argument. The author never coaches the reaction.

The reader's response is the reader's business. A fact that is disturbing is disturbing without being labelled so. If a quotation is damning, quote it and move on; the sentence "read that twice" adds nothing to the sentence it points at, and subtracts authority from the writer who needed to say it.

**Banned outright:**

- Second person. No *you*, no *your*, no rhetorical questions to the reader.
- First-person staging. No *I want you to*, *I need you to understand*, *I can feel how that reads*, *I'll handle this with tongs*, *let me tell you*.
- Reaction instructions. No *sit with that*, *read that twice*, *notice what*, *hold onto that*, *keep reading*, *ought to make your neck prickle*.
- Throat-clearing. No *start with the concession, because…*, *before I go on*, *this essay will argue that*. Begin with the claim.
- Closing reassurance. No *you don't need a secret to be disturbed by that.*

The bare first person is permitted only where the author must state a methodological commitment that has no other grammatical home ("no boardroom is claimed"), and even there the impersonal construction is preferred.

## 2. Open on the claim, not on the frame

The first sentence of a piece does work. It states something true and consequential about the subject. It does not announce the piece's structure, apologize for its ambition, or explain why the reader should continue.

> **Before:** "Start with the concession, because the concession is the whole reason you should keep reading."
>
> **After:** "Most of the managed child was built by people with no strange convictions at all."

## 3. The disciplines are findings, not confessions

The project's honesty rules — the denominator, symptom-not-cause, association-is-not-coordination, testimony-not-cause — are **substantive claims about the evidence.** Write them as conclusions the record forces, never as the author's anxious self-policing. A constraint stated as a confession invites the reader to doubt the writer. The same constraint stated as a finding makes the reader trust the work.

> **Before:** "I can feel how that reads. It reads like the smoking gun — the secret table where they carved up the century. And this is exactly the point at which a lesser version of this argument gives itself away…"
>
> **After:** "The table looks like coordination, and it was not."

> **Before:** "I need you to understand what that pamphlet is, because it is the reason this whole dry rail matters."
>
> **After:** "*UNESCO: Its Purpose and Its Philosophy* is not a critic's paraphrase; it is Huxley, over his own agency's imprint, defining what the organization is for."

## 4. Certainty lives in the sourcing phrase, never in a bracket

The apparatus (grades, tags, node-numbers, `[PRIMARY]`, WELD / HYPOTHESIS / FICTION-ALERT) is scaffolding for the graph and is **stripped entirely** from prose. Load-bearing certainty is carried instead by ordinary English that reports provenance:

- *published under his own name* · *on his own agency's imprint* · *in the participants' own memoirs* · *verbatim*
- *no documented tie exists* · *the record does not contain that room* · *the defensible claim is narrow*
- *stands as testimony, not cause* · *a symptom of the same root in another theater*

A reader must be able to feel the load-rating of every sentence without ever seeing the word that assigns it.

## 5. Adjectives may rate evidence. They may not rate emotions.

*Damning*, *unassailable*, *documented*, *narrow*, *sufficient* describe the evidentiary weight of a thing — permitted, sparingly. *Chilling*, *disturbing*, *shocking*, *sinister* describe how the reader is supposed to feel — banned. Let the fact be alarming; do not call it alarming.

## 6. Paragraph shape: claim → evidence → constraint

The characteristic move of this project is to make a strong claim, prove it from named sources, and then immediately fence it. The fence is part of the argument, in the same paragraph or the one following, in the same declarative voice.

Huxley's UNESCO sentence is quoted, then bounded: the pamphlet is his stated philosophy, not ratified UN policy — *and it does not need to be, because the founder signed it.* The Coefficients guest list is given, then bounded: Russell resigned in 1903 — *the same roster that proves contact proves disagreement.* The fence never weakens the claim; it converts the claim into one a hostile reader cannot take away.

## 7. Discards are dispatched in three sentences

State the circulating falsehood, name it false, give the narrow true version, move on. No hedging, no relish, no paragraph of throat-clearing about intellectual honesty.

> "The story that Aleister Crowley introduced Wells to hashish belongs to the genre of initiation-legend that accretes around famous men and is false. No documented intellectual tie between Wells and Crowley exists. The defensible claim is narrow — the same city and literary decade, nothing more."

## 8. Quotation

Provenance goes in the sentence *before* the quotation, so the reader knows the document's authority as they read its words. Load-bearing quotations are set as blockquotes and reproduced **verbatim**, including the source's own capitalization. Never paraphrase a quotation that is doing evidentiary work, and never introduce one with an instruction to admire it.

## 9. Connect the dots explicitly

The project asks for connection, and timidity is its own failure mode. Where a chain of encounter exists, *state it as a chain* — plainly, in one sentence, name to name: "The naturalist taught the technocrat; the technocrat later co-wrote a book with the naturalist's grandson; the grandson is where the rail terminates." Confidence is earned by the grading discipline, so spend it. What is forbidden is asserting a connection the graph grades as unproven — not asserting one it grades as documented.

## 10. Cadence

Declarative sentences. Concrete nouns. End paragraphs on the hard fact, not on a flourish. Vary length, but let the shortest sentence carry the heaviest load — that is where the argument lands.

> "The founding document is on the shelf."

---

## The test

Read the draft and strike every sentence that would still be true if the evidence were different. If a sentence survives only because it manages the reader — orienting, reassuring, instructing, exciting — it is not in this register, and it goes.

Then ask the two questions the merge gate asks: does every load-bearing sentence trace to a named source, and has any apparatus leaked? If the prose passes all three, it is in voice.
