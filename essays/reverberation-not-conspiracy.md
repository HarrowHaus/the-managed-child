# A Great Deal of Reading

[Christopher Vogler](../data/nodes/people/vogler-christopher.md) never met [Joseph Campbell](../data/nodes/people/campbell-joseph.md). He read him.

In 1985, a story analyst at Walt Disney, Vogler took a book Campbell had published thirty-six years earlier — *[The Hero with a Thousand Faces](../data/nodes/works/hero-thousand-faces.md)* — and compressed its pattern into a seven-page memo for screenwriters. The memo spread through the studios, grew into a book of its own, and the shape it carried now underlies a large share of what Hollywood releases. Between the mythologist and the industry there was a shelf, a paperback, and a reader who did the rest alone.

[Edward Bernays](../data/nodes/people/bernays-edward.md) did not have to reach for his source; he was born to it. Bernays was [Sigmund Freud](../data/nodes/people/freud-sigmund.md)'s nephew, and he carried the family idea — that a person is moved by an unconscious he cannot see — out of the consulting room and sold it to corporations as a method for shifting goods. The line from the Vienna clinic to the New York account is as plain as a bloodline and a bibliography: an ambitious nephew read a famous uncle and found a use he could bill for.

[H. G. Wells](../data/nodes/people/wells-hg.md) learned his biology in a classroom. In 1884, a scholarship student of nineteen at the Normal School of Science in South Kensington, he sat through the lectures of [Thomas Henry Huxley](../data/nodes/people/huxley-th.md), the most prominent naturalist of the age; four decades later he co-wrote a three-volume popular biology with Huxley's grandson Julian. Teacher to student, then author to author — each link dated, each on the record.

A single figure keeps surfacing across seventy years: the child enthroned as the ruler of a coming age. [Crowley](../data/nodes/people/crowley-aleister.md) set it into a received book in Cairo in 1904. [Annie Besant](../data/nodes/people/besant-annie.md) built an institution around a living boy at Madras in 1911. [Alice Bailey](../data/nodes/people/bailey-alice.md), who had walked out of the [Theosophy](../data/nodes/institutions/theosophical-society.md) that Besant led, chartered a trust for a guiding hierarchy in New Jersey in 1922. [Puharich](../data/nodes/people/puharich-andrija.md)'s circle transcribed a voice that called itself [the Nine](../data/nodes/institutions/lab-nine.md) in the 1970s. Between the four runs no thread: Besant and Crowley were rivals who despised each other; Bailey left Besant's movement rather than carry it on; Puharich's people had never heard of any of the three. The figure appears the way one melody turns up in four composers who never met — something in the air of a period, caught separately.

[Francis Galton](../data/nodes/people/galton-francis.md) coined "eugenics" in London in 1883 and built the arithmetic to rank a population. Here, unlike the four idioms, the record *does* carry a line — but an open, published one. Galton's protégé and biographer [Karl Pearson](../data/nodes/people/pearson-karl.md) made the mathematics exact; Galton's postdoctoral student James McKeen Cattell coined "mental test" in 1890 and carried the measurement to America; Cattell's own doctoral student Edward Thorndike built the Columbia school of educational measurement. [Lewis Terman](../data/nodes/people/terman-lewis.md), who set a ranking test into American schools, stands a little to the side of that teacher-to-student line — his doctorate was under [G. Stanley Hall](../data/nodes/people/hall-g-stanley.md) — yet he cited Galton by name, building his *Genetic Studies of Genius* directly out of Galton's *Hereditary Genius*. So this is not merely a shared climate any reader breathes: it is a named, datable genealogy — Galton → Cattell → Thorndike — with Terman invoking Galton directly. What the record still refuses is a conspiracy or a secret relay. The descent is branched, open, and published — transmission by training and citation, the ordinary machinery of a discipline, not a cabal.

The one place the record actually seats these people at a single table did not hold. The Webbs founded [the Coefficients](../data/nodes/institutions/coefficients-club.md) in 1902 as a dining club that, on the members' own later record, mixed the managerial left with the imperial right, once a month. [Bertrand Russell](../data/nodes/people/russell-bertrand.md) resigned inside a year over another member's foreign policy; Wells stayed only long enough to argue with the majority, then fell away; the club was gone within the decade. Wells had already made his own attempt, in 1906, to seize the [Fabian Society](../data/nodes/institutions/fabian-society.md); he lost a public debate to [Bernard Shaw](../data/nodes/people/shaw-george-bernard.md) and resigned two years later. And when [John Dewey](../data/nodes/people/dewey-john.md) took up [Walter Lippmann](../data/nodes/people/lippmann-walter.md)'s two books on steering the public, he reviewed them with admiration — and then wrote one of his own against their central proposal.

A book read in an office, an idea taken from an uncle, a lecture heard at nineteen, one figure reached for by four strangers, a climate breathed from a field, a dinner club that broke up over its diners. A great deal of reading, and very little agreement.
