## TL;DR
- **The person and the record are REAL and daylight.** Beatrice Potter (b. 22 Jan 1858, Gloucestershire; d. 30 Apr 1943) married **Sidney Webb in 1892**; the pair became the twentieth century's model social-investigator partnership. She co-founded the **London School of Economics (1895)** and the **New Statesman (1913)**, sat on the **Royal Commission on the Poor Laws (1905–1909)** and was **lead author of its dissenting Minority Report (1909)**, and wrote *My Apprenticeship* (1926), the posthumous *Our Partnership* (1948), and — with Sidney — *Industrial Democracy* (1897) and *Soviet Communism: A New Civilisation?* (1935). **Grade the node WELD.**
- **She states the administrative-elite doctrine IN HER OWN HAND.** Her diary (comparing herself and Sidney with the Liberal democrat Leonard Courtney): *"we have little faith in the 'average sensual man,' we do not believe that he can do much more than describe his grievances, we do not think that he can prescribe the remedies"* — and, the load-bearing line, *"We wish to introduce into politics the professional expert — to extend the sphere of government by adding to its enormous advantages of wholesale and compulsory management, the advantage of the most skilled entrepreneur."* This is `rule-by-trained-elite`/`the-root` in Beatrice's own words: the fit expert governs; the ordinary man may describe his grievance but not prescribe.
- **The "national minimum" is the welfare-state-by-experts, and it is hers.** The Minority Report proposed to abolish the Poor Law and replace it with specialized expert authorities securing *"a national minimum of civilised life … open to all alike … sufficient nourishment and training when young, a living wage when able-bodied, treatment when sick, and modest but secure livelihood when disabled or aged"* (Beatrice's own summary, *Our Partnership*). The doctrinal root of the phrase is *Industrial Democracy* (1897): *"extend the conception of the Common Rule from the trade to the whole community … by prescribing a National Minimum."* The administered society, of which the administered child is the domestic case.
- **The eugenics adjacency is real but must be attributed with a scalpel (R-3).** Beatrice recorded her belief that eugenics was *"the most important question of all"* (diary) — that is documented **as hers**. But the notorious passage about *"the less thrifty, the less intellectual, the less far-seeing races or classes … the negroes, the Chinese"* is **SIDNEY's**, from *The Decline in the Birth-Rate* (Fabian Tract 131, 1907) — do **NOT** put it in Beatrice's mouth. The shared concern was **"national efficiency"** and the differential birth-rate; the specific race-suicide wording is Sidney's tract. Attribute each line to its actual author.
- **The 1930s Soviet firewall is documented, attributed, and kept from swallowing the node (R-3).** The Webbs' *Soviet Communism: A New Civilisation?* (Longmans, 1935) defended Stalin's USSR; the **question-mark was dropped from the title in the 1937 second edition** — the datable emblem of their hardening credulity. This is documented and belongs on the node, but as one late chapter, precisely dated, not the node's center of gravity (S-5, R-3).
- **The stub's own paraphrase is unverified.** The current stub line *"breeding the right sort of man"* is **not located verbatim** as Beatrice's. The closest verified items are her diary's *"most important question of all"* (eugenics, hers) and Sidney's 1907 tract (his). Report the real ones; do not carry the stub's paraphrase in quotation marks.

## The PRIMARIES

**The diaries and the autobiographies (the load-bearing self-testimony).**
- *My Apprenticeship* (Longmans, Green, **1926**) — her autobiography to 1892 (her method as social investigator; the turn to socialism). Public-scholarly; reissued Cambridge UP 1979.
- *Our Partnership* (Longmans, Green, **1948**, posthumous; ed. Barbara Drake & Margaret Cole) — covers 1892–1911; contains her retrospective statement of the Minority Report's aim (the "national minimum" summary quoted below).
- *The Diary of Beatrice Webb*, 4 vols., ed. Norman & Jeanne MacKenzie (Virago/LSE, 1982–1985); the digitized manuscript diary is held and imaged by the **LSE Digital Library** (Passfield Papers). The "professional expert" / "average sensual man" passage is a diary entry (mid-1880s, the Courtney comparison), quoted verbatim in Gertrude Himmelfarb's "Winston vs. the Webbs" and in the standard biographies. **L2/R-2 caveat:** verify the wording against the MacKenzie diary edition or the LSE manuscript before rendering it `documented`; this harvest confirms the wording across independent secondary hosts but the citable primary is the diary edition.

**The Fabian / social-science corpus (co-authored with Sidney).**
- *The History of Trade Unionism* (1894) and ***Industrial Democracy*** (2 vols., **1897**) — the Webbs jointly; *Industrial Democracy* is the source of the **"National Minimum"** and **"Common Rule"** doctrine (Vol. II, pp. 766–767): *"The remedy is to extend the conception of the Common Rule from the trade to the whole community, and by prescribing a National Minimum."*
- *English Local Government* (multi-volume, 1906–1929) — the Webbs jointly; the historical scaffolding of the Poor Law campaign.

**The Poor Law Minority Report (the expert-administered welfare state).**
- Royal Commission on the Poor Laws and Relief of Distress, **1905–1909**; Beatrice appointed 1905, served ~four years, and was **lead author of the dissenting Minority Report (1909)** (Sidney drafted alongside; the young **William Beveridge** and **R. H. Tawney** assisted the Webbs' campaign). The Report proposed to **"break up"** the Poor Law and distribute its functions to specialized expert committees (health, children, the aged, the unemployed) — administration by trained officials, not the deterrent workhouse. Public-domain: Internet Archive (`b28121168`), HathiTrust, Wellcome Collection. The Webbs took it to the public in the **National Committee for the Prevention of Destitution** (1909–). This is the direct institutional ancestor the standard narrative draws to the 1942 Beveridge Report and the welfare state.

**The institutions she co-founded.**
- **London School of Economics and Political Science (LSE), 1895** — founded from the Henry Hutchinson bequest (~£10,000) with Sidney, **Graham Wallas**, and **G. B. Shaw**; the Fabians' instrument for training the administrative class in the social sciences. (No `lse` node on disk — flag, do not create.)
- **The *New Statesman*, 1913** — co-founded with Sidney and Henry D. Harben, first editor Clifford Sharp; the Fabian weekly. (No `new-statesman` node — flag.)
- **The Coefficients dining club, 1902** — founded with Sidney (already on `webb-sidney` and `rule-by-trained-elite`); seats the managerial left and imperial right at one table.

**The Soviet turn (charged, dated, contained).**
- ***Soviet Communism: A New Civilisation?*** (Longmans, Green, **1935**), with Sidney — a two-volume defense of the USSR after their 1932 visit. **The question-mark was dropped from the title in the 1937 second edition** (and the Left Book Club/later printings), signaling their hardened endorsement even as the Great Terror ran. A 1941 edition's postscript reassured readers that in 1937 "strenuous efforts had been made … to cut out the deadwood." Documented; keep precise (R-3).

## The eugenics / "national efficiency" adjacency (R-3 split — attribute with a scalpel)

The Webbs belonged to the Edwardian **"national efficiency"** milieu, anxious about the differential birth-rate (fewer children among the thrifty/educated, more among the poor) as a threat to the race and the empire. This is `network-exposure` / `common-inheritance` in the milieu and is documented — but the two spouses' words must not be merged:

- **SIDNEY (not Beatrice):** *The Decline in the Birth-Rate*, **Fabian Tract No. 131 (March 1907)** — the tract warning that Britain was being "**recruited from the . . . the less thrifty, the less intellectual, the less far-seeing races or classes**," naming *"the unskilled casual labourers of our greater cities, the races of Eastern or Southern Europe, the negroes, the Chinese."* **This is Sidney's tract.** Wire it to `webb-sidney` (or a eugenics cluster per R-Q1), **never** put the "negroes, the Chinese" wording in Beatrice's mouth. (The prompt's L2 warning; verified: Fabian Tract 131 is authored by Sidney Webb.)
- **BEATRICE (as hers):** her diary judgment that eugenics was *"the most important question of all,"* and her documented interest (she met the eugenicist Dr. Caleb Saleeby). This is the tie that attaches to *her* node — her endorsement of eugenics as a question, not the specific racial catalogue.
- **The endowment-of-motherhood / national-minimum register** is where the two meet: raising the birth-rate among the fit and securing the minimum for all children is the same administrative impulse (the administered society reaching the cradle). Wire this as `restates`/`analogy` toward `the-root` and the managed-child, **not** as a documented line into any child agency.

## L2 dating / attribution spine (the traps)

- **Beatrice did NOT contribute to *Fabian Essays in Socialism* (1889).** That volume's seven contributors were Shaw, **Sidney** Webb, Clarke, Olivier, Wallas, Besant, Bland (see `fabian-essays`). Beatrice had not yet met Sidney (they met c. 1890) or married him (1892) and was not a Fabian in 1889. **Do not** list her among the essayists; her Fabian entry is *through the partnership from 1892*, not the 1889 book.
- **The 1907 birth-rate / race-suicide tract is SIDNEY's**, not Beatrice's (above).
- **"The inevitability of gradualness" is SIDNEY's** 1923 Labour Party address (Fabian Tract 207), not Beatrice's.
- **The Soviet question-mark dropped in 1937**, not 1935 — the first edition (1935) *has* the question-mark.
- **Industrial Democracy (1897) and Soviet Communism (1935) are CO-authored** — render the partnership as `co-authored`, not as Beatrice's sole work.
