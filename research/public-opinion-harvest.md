## The PRIMARY — *Public Opinion* (Walter Lippmann, 1922)

**Title:** *Public Opinion.*
**Author:** **Walter Lippmann** (1889–1974).
**Publisher / year (confirmed):** **Harcourt, Brace and Company, New York, 1922.** (Simultaneous/near London printing by George Allen & Unwin.)
**Structure:** eight Parts, 28 chapters. **Part I** opens with **Chapter I, "The World Outside and the Pictures in Our Heads."** **Part V, "The Making of a Common Will,"** contains **Chapter XV, "Leaders and the Rank and File,"** where "the manufacture of consent" appears in the closing argument. **Part VIII, "Organized Intelligence,"** carries the expert-class / intelligence-bureau remedy.
**Full text (public domain — verbatim-check, orientation):** Project Gutenberg #6456, https://www.gutenberg.org/cache/epub/6456/pg6456-images.html ; Wikisource, https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Public_Opinion ; scanned PDF (Univ. of Athens eclass), https://eclass.uoa.gr/modules/document/file.php/MEDIA237/book%20Lippmann%20Public%20Opinion.pdf

### Verbatim lines that state the doctrine [PRIMARY — public-domain text]

- **Chapter I title / thesis frame:** **"The World Outside and the Pictures in Our Heads."** The chapter argues the citizen acts not on the world but on a mental image of it: *"The pictures inside the heads of these human beings, the pictures of themselves, of others, of their needs, purposes, and relationship, are their public opinions."*
- **The pseudo-environment:** man's *"behavior is a response to the pseudo-environment"* — *"For the most part we do not first see, and then define, we define first and then see."* (The stereotype apparatus: we perceive through pre-fixed pictures.) Lippmann: the pseudo-environment is *"a hybrid compounded of 'human nature' and 'conditions.'"*
- **The manufacture of consent [Chapter XV, "Leaders and the Rank and File"]:** **"That the manufacture of consent is capable of great refinements no one, I think, denies. The process by which public opinions arise is certainly no less intricate than it has appeared in these pages, and the opportunities for manipulation open to anyone who understands the process are plain enough."**
- **The same passage — the "revolution" claim (the thesis's core):** **"Within the life of the generation now in control of affairs, persuasion has become a self-conscious art and a regular organ of popular government. None of us begins to understand the consequences, but it is no daring prophecy to say that the knowledge of how to create consent will alter every political calculation and modify every political premise."** And: **"as a result of psychological research, coupled with the modern means of communication, the practice of democracy has turned a corner. A revolution is taking place, infinitely more significant than any shifting of economic power."**
- **The specialized/expert class [Part VIII]:** the public's incompetence requires **"a specialized class whose interests reach beyond the locality"** — men who *"see the world"* the ordinary citizen cannot. Lippmann's remedy is **"intelligence bureaus"**: *"representative government… cannot be worked successfully, no matter what the basis of election, unless there is an independent, expert organization for making the unseen facts intelligible to those who have to make the decisions."*
- **The public's incapacity (the diagnosis under the remedy):** the citizen is a spectator of a world he cannot see whole; *"the real environment is altogether too big, too complex, and too fleeting for direct acquaintance,"* so he governs by fictions. This is the administrable-public premise: a public that cannot know cannot rule, and so must be managed.

### The companion — *The Phantom Public* (1925) [PRIMARY — the sharpening, kept DISTINCT from 1922]

**Title / publisher:** *The Phantom Public*, **Harcourt, Brace and Company, 1925** (first edition confirmed via trade catalogues). The disillusioned sequel, where the "phantom" name and the citizen-as-spectator argument harden.

- **The deaf spectator [the signature line]:** **"The private citizen today has come to feel rather like a deaf spectator in the back row, who ought to keep his mind on the mystery off there, but cannot quite manage to keep awake. He knows he is somehow affected by what is going on. Rules and regulations continually, taxes annually and wars occasionally remind him that he is being swept along by great drifts of circumstance. Yet these public affairs are in no convincing way his affairs."**
- **The public as phantom / "bewildered herd":** the sovereign public of democratic theory is *"a mere phantom,"* an abstraction; the individual citizen is *"a deaf spectator,"* one of a **"bewildered herd."** Governance, on this account, cannot rest on the public's competence.

**Attribution discipline (L2):** keep the two books apart. *"The world outside and the pictures in our heads,"* *"pseudo-environment,"* and *"the manufacture of consent"* are **1922** (*Public Opinion*). *"Deaf spectator in the back row,"* *"phantom,"* *"bewildered herd"* are **1925** (*The Phantom Public*). Do not cite the 1925 phrases to the 1922 book or vice versa.

## The Bernays tie and the Creel/CPI overlap (R-3 split; documented context, no conspiracy)

**Documented reception — Bernays worked off Lippmann [network/direct-transmission].** Edward Bernays read Lippmann's *Public Opinion* and drew on it in his own *Crystallizing Public Opinion* (1923); his later essay **"The Engineering of Consent"** (*Annals of the American Academy of Political and Social Science*, March 1947) echoes Lippmann's phrase. Bernays's stated influences run to Le Bon, Trotter, Freud, and Lippmann — the herd-and-management canon. **The connection is documented; the intent is not shared or coordinated** (R-3): Lippmann was a theorist-critic of democracy, Bernays a working publicist; "manufacture" (Lippmann, analysis, 1922) and "engineering" (Bernays, trade, 1947) are adjacent, not identical, and the chronology forbids reading Bernays as Lippmann's source. This confirms the EDGE-VOCABULARY "Known fix": the arrow runs Bernays← Lippmann for reading, Lippmann for the coinage.

**Documented institutional overlap — the Committee on Public Information [institutional-relay].** Both men worked in the WWI federal propaganda apparatus. **Bernays** served in the **Committee on Public Information (the "Creel Committee," April 1917–Aug 1919)**, in its Latin American / foreign-press section. **Lippmann** urged Wilson toward an official propaganda body in 1917 and was associated with Allied/CPI propaganda and intelligence work in Europe in 1918 (he later became a sharp critic of George Creel). Co-presence in one institution is `institutional-relay` at most — it documents a **shared milieu that carried the persuasion craft**, and it must never be read as coordination or a cabal (EDGE-VOCAB `institutional-relay` "does-not-permit": co-membership is not agreement). **Node candidates flagged:** neither `creel`/`committee-on-public-information` nor `wallas` (Graham Wallas, Lippmann's teacher and author of *Human Nature in Politics*, 1908, and *The Great Society*, 1914 — to which *Public Opinion* is dedicated) exists in `data/INDEX.md`; both are strong future nodes but are NOT to be invented here.

**The Lippmann–Dewey counter [wire to existing node].** Dewey reviewed *Public Opinion* and *The Phantom Public* favourably and answered the technocratic remedy in *The Public and Its Problems* (1927): the public was "in eclipse," to be reconstructed, not replaced by experts. A node already exists — `lippmann-dewey-debate` (WELD) — and already corrects the "great debate" framing as a later scholarly construction.
