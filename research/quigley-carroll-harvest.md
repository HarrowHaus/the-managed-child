### 1. *Tragedy and Hope: A History of the World in Our Time* (Macmillan, 1966) [PRIMARY — verbatim, page-checked]

**Full title:** *Tragedy and Hope: A History of the World in Our Time.*
**Author:** **Carroll Quigley.**
**Publisher / date:** **The Macmillan Company, New York, 1966** (~1,300 pp.).
**Full text (verbatim-check hosts):** Internet Archive (`tragedy-hope-a-history-of-the-world-in-our-time-carroll-quigley`; also `TragedyAndHope_501`); `carrollquigley.net`.

- **The financial-capitalism / BIS passage — p. 324 [PRIMARY, verbatim]:** *"The powers of financial capitalism had another far-reaching aim, nothing less than to create a world system of financial control in private hands able to dominate the political system of each country and the economy of the world as a whole. This system was to be controlled in a feudalist fashion by the central banks of the world acting in concert, by secret agreements arrived at in frequent private meetings and conferences. The apex of the system was to be the Bank for International Settlements in Basle, Switzerland, a private bank owned and controlled by the world's central banks which were themselves private corporations."* **L2 / R-8 note:** this is Quigley's *institutional description* of the central-banking apparatus's ambition (the BIS/Basel system) — a historian characterizing a documented financial architecture. The conspiracy corpus rips it from that context to "prove" a secret-cabal world takeover. Quote it in context, as a description of central-bank coordination, never as the project asserting a cabal.

- **The "international Anglophile network" passage — ~p. 950 [PRIMARY, verbatim] — LOAD-BEARING:** *"There does exist, and has existed for a generation, an international Anglophile network which operates, to some extent, in the way the radical Right believes the Communists act. I know of the operations of this network because I have studied it for twenty years and was permitted for two years, in the early 1960's, to examine its papers and secret records. I have no aversion to it nor to most of its aims and have, for much of my life, been close to it and to many of its instruments. I have objected, both in the past and recently, to a few of its policies … but in general my chief difference of opinion is that it wishes to remain unknown, and I believe its role in history is significant enough to be known."* This is the node's spine: Quigley (i) affirms the network **exists**, (ii) affirms he **documented it from its own records**, (iii) affirms he is **sympathetic to its aims**, and (iv) objects **only to its secrecy**. He is an insider-critic of *concealment*, not an exposer of *evil intent*.

### 2. *The Anglo-American Establishment: From Rhodes to Cliveden* (written 1949, pub. 1981) [PRIMARY — verbatim, archive-checked]

**Full title:** *The Anglo-American Establishment: From Rhodes to Cliveden.*
**Author:** **Carroll Quigley.**
**Written:** c. **1949**; **published posthumously 1981**, **Books in Focus, New York** (ISBN 0-916728-50-1). The more scholarly, tightly-sourced monograph; *Tragedy and Hope* is the vast general history, this the focused study.
**Full text:** Internet Archive (`pdfy-A7-BNmZpG-RLOXZZ`); `carrollquigley.net/pdf/The_Anglo-American_Establishment.pdf`.

- **The founding, verbatim opening [PRIMARY]:** *"One wintry afternoon in February 1891, three men were engaged in earnest conversation in London. From that conversation were to flow consequences of the greatest importance to the British Empire and to the world as a whole."* The three: **Cecil Rhodes, William T. Stead, and Reginald Baliol Brett (Lord Esher)**; **Alfred Milner** added shortly after. Date given as **5 February 1891**.
- **The structure, verbatim [PRIMARY]:** the plan established *"an inner circle, to be known as 'The Society of the Elect,' and an outer circle, to be known as 'The Association of Helpers.'"* Real power in the leader and a small junta (Rhodes, Stead, Brett/Esher, then Milner).
- **The continuity claim [PRIMARY]:** *"this secret society was created by Rhodes and his principal trustee, Lord Milner, and continues to exist to this day."* Traced through **Milner's Kindergarten** (1901–1910), the **Round Table Group** and journal (1910–1920), and later **"The Times crowd," the "Chatham House crowd" (Royal Institute of International Affairs), the "All Souls group," and the "Cliveden set."**
- **What the book IS:** a documented history of a real imperial-federation network — Rhodes's wills, Milner's proteges, the Round Table journal, Chatham House, All Souls, The Times — assembled from records. **What it is NOT:** a bloodline theory, an Illuminati exposé, or a claim of a communist-banker plot. The word "secret society" is Quigley's, and it means *a network that preferred to work unseen* — his complaint (T&H p.950) is precisely that it should be **known**.

### 3. Biography [near-primary — cross-checked]
Professor at **Georgetown University's School of Foreign Service, 1941–1976**; celebrated two-semester "Development of Civilizations" course; also taught at Princeton and Harvard; consultant to the Congressional Select Committee on Astronautics and Space (Smithsonian). Student **Bill Clinton** (course 1965; cited Quigley in his 1991 campaign-launch address). Died **3 January 1977** (heart attack, Georgetown University Hospital). Author also of *The Evolution of Civilizations* (1961) and *Weapons Systems and Political Stability* (posthumous). [documented — Wikipedia; Georgetown; Scientia Press]
