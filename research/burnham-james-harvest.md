## The PRIMARIES

### 1. *The Managerial Revolution: What Is Happening in the World* (John Day, 1941) [PRIMARY — verbatim, dated] — the naming of the managerial class

**Full title:** *The Managerial Revolution: What Is Happening in the World.*
**Author:** James Burnham. **Publisher/date:** **The John Day Company, New York, 1941.**

- The thesis, verbatim: **"Capitalist society will be replaced by 'managerial society,' … the transition from capitalist society to managerial society is already well under way."** (p. 7.)
- The shift of control, verbatim: **"control over the instruments of production is everywhere undergoing a shift, away from the capitalists proper and toward the managers."** (p. 29.) Burnham's definition: the fundamental task of **"direction and co-ordination"** he calls *managing*, and those who do it *managers* — those who manage the actual processes of production.
- Rule through the bureau, verbatim: **"In a new form of society, sovereignty is localized in administrative bureaus. … The active heads of the bureaus are the managers-in-government, the same, or nearly the same, in training, functions, skills, habits of thought as the managers-in-industry."**
- One revolution, three regimes, verbatim: **"the New Deal moves in the same direction as Stalinism and Nazism"** (p. 203); the Russian revolution was **"not a socialist revolution … but a managerial revolution,"** and Russia is **"the nation which has, in its structural aspects, advanced furthest along the managerial road"** (pp. 220–221).
- The break with the socialist prediction (from his own 1940 *Letter of Resignation* from the Workers Party, the seed of the book): a new **"exploitive society (which I call 'managerial society') is not only possible but is a more probable outcome"** than socialism.

This is the load-bearing primary for the node's map-3.5 job: Burnham names "the managers" and "managerial society" as the successor ruling class. Public-domain-adjacent; full text widely archived (Internet Archive; John Day 1941 first edition).

### 2. *The Machiavellians: Defenders of Freedom* (John Day, 1943) [PRIMARY — verbatim, dated] — the elite-theory bridge

**Full title:** *The Machiavellians: Defenders of Freedom.*
**Author:** James Burnham. **Publisher/date:** **The John Day Company, New York, 1943.** (Internet Archive: `BurnhamJamesTheMachiavellians`.)

Burnham's popularization of the Italian elite-theory school — **Machiavelli**, **Gaetano Mosca**, **Vilfredo Pareto**, **Robert Michels**, and **Georges Sorel** (with a chapter on **Dante** as the "myth of the whole"). The book's spine is the thesis at the heart of `rule-by-trained-elite`:

- **The minority-rule thesis, the book's core:** **"all societies … are ruled by a minority"** — no society, including those called democratic, is governed by the people or by a majority; every society divides into a **"ruling class,"** always a minority (the **"elite"**), and the **"ruled."** (Mosca's discovery, which Burnham adopts as the primary universal social fact.)
- **Mosca — the "political formula":** the ruling minority always seeks to **justify and legitimize its rule through a "formula"** — the myth or ideology (e.g. "the will of the people," "divine right") that dresses the elite's real interest in maintaining power. Burnham makes this the mechanism by which elites disguise rule as representation.
- **Michels — the iron law of oligarchy:** verbatim in the book, **"the state cannot be anything other than the organization of a minority"**; and **"The fact of leadership, obscured by the theory of representation, negates the principle of democracy."**
- **Pareto — the circulation of elites:** revolutions do not abolish elite rule; they **circulate** one elite out and another in — "if the ruled revolt and succeed, it is not the people who come to power but the leaders of the people, a new elite replacing an old."
- **Machiavelli — the method:** **"Politics is nothing more than the struggle for power,"** studied without moralizing; Burnham praises Machiavelli for treating **formal and real meaning as one** ("in all scientific writing there is no such distinction … open and explicit"), and defends him against the "official thinkers … lawyers and philosophers and preachers … [who] must defame Machiavelli."

**This is the text that answers the trunk node's held-open flag.** `rule-by-trained-elite.md` carries a HYPOTHESIS edge and a "Machiavelli → Burnham … the text has not been read against the claim" note; the map's claim was that *The Machiavellians* (1943) is the bridge carrying Machiavelli into the managerial-elite thesis. **Read: it holds.** The book IS Burnham's explicit popularization of the elite-theorists and the frame he'd already stated (1941) in managerial terms. The flag can be lifted (see Build note).

### 3. The Orwell tie — "James Burnham and the Managerial Revolution" (1946) + *Nineteen Eighty-Four* (1949) [PRIMARY / documented — Orwell as CRITIC]

- **The essay:** George Orwell, **"Second Thoughts on James Burnham,"** first published in ***Polemic*** No. 3, **May 1946**; reissued the same year as a Socialist Book Centre pamphlet titled ***James Burnham and the Managerial Revolution*** (London, 1946).
- **Orwell's paraphrase of Burnham (documented summary):** capitalism disappears but socialism does not replace it; instead a **"managerial" society** emerges in which **"business executives, technicians, bureaucrats and soldiers"** rule as an oligarchy, organized into **"great super-states grouped round the main industrial centres in Europe, Asia, and America,"** each internally stratified with managers on top and masses below.
- **Orwell as CRITIC, verbatim:** he diagnoses Burnham's **"power worship"** — **"Whoever is winning at the moment will always seem to be invincible … the instinct to bow down before the conqueror of the moment."** Orwell shows Burnham's forecast tracking whoever was ascendant (German victory in 1940, Russian in 1945) — "predicting a continuation of the thing that is happening" — and judges the "objective" theory to conceal a wish for a hierarchical society. **Document the tie as influence-through-critique; Orwell rejected Burnham's power-worship, not the geographic picture, which he granted had "turned out to be correct" (1945).**
- ***Nineteen Eighty-Four* (Secker & Warburg, 1949):** the world of three perpetually warring super-states (Oceania, Eurasia, Eastasia) and the smuggled in-novel treatise, **Emmanuel Goldstein's *The Theory and Practice of Oligarchical Collectivism***, draw on Burnham's managerial thesis. The scholar George Kateb argued Orwell's reading of Burnham furnished **"the intellectual basis of 1984."** (Adjacent to `huxley-aldous`, whose 1949 letter to Orwell about *Nineteen Eighty-Four* is already in the corpus — the Huxley/Orwell axis.)

### 4. The biographical trajectory (R-3, kept precise and dated) [documented — biographical, cross-checked]

- **Trotskyist period:** co-organized the **American Workers Party** with Sidney Hook (1933); allied with the Trotskyist wing (1935); a friend and correspondent of **Leon Trotsky**. Broke with dialectical materialism in **"Science and Style: A Reply to Comrade Trotsky"** (Feb 1940); **resigned the Workers Party on 21 May 1940**, and rejected Marxism — the pivot that produced *The Managerial Revolution* a year later. [documented — Wikipedia "James Burnham," cross-checked with Daniel Kelly, *James Burnham and the Struggle for the World* (2002)]
- **WWII:** took leave from NYU to work for the **Office of Strategic Services (OSS)**. [documented]
- **CIA / OPC (the fact to keep documented, dated, non-conspiratorial):** Burnham **secretly joined the CIA's Office of Policy Coordination (OPC) as a full-time consultant in October 1949**, recruited by **Joseph J. Bryan III**, a former Princeton classmate who headed the OPC's **Psychological Warfare Workshop**; he was associated with the origins of the **Congress for Cultural Freedom** (1950). His anti-communist zeal was reportedly so extreme the CIA later cut him off. [documented — Wikipedia "James Burnham"; Kelly biography; Powerbase]
- ***National Review*:** **co-founded the magazine with William F. Buckley Jr. in 1955**, serving as an editor until his death in **1987**. Buckley called him **"the number one intellectual influence on *National Review* since the day of its founding."** [documented]
- **Downstream on the right:** his "managerial revolution" and elite theory were the base of **Sam Francis**'s (1947–2005) **"managerial state"** critique — two books on Burnham — and, through Francis, of paleo- and New-Right "anti-managerial" politics. Received the Presidential Medal of Freedom (1983). [documented — Wikipedia; *American Affairs*; Cornell UP intellectual biography]
