## The PRIMARY — *Fabian Essays in Socialism* (ed. G. B. Shaw, Fabian Society, 1889)

**Full title:** *Fabian Essays in Socialism.*
**Editor:** **George Bernard Shaw** (who also wrote the Preface to the English edition and two of the eight essays).
**Publisher / date:** the **Fabian Society**, London, **December 1889** (first edition). US edition 1891, with a Preface to the American edition by **H. G. Wilshire**.
**Public-domain full text (verbatim-check hosts):** Internet Archive, `https://archive.org/details/fabianessaysinso00fabirich` (djvu full text at `/stream/fabianessaysinso00fabirich/fabianessaysinso00fabirich_djvu.txt`); Project Gutenberg #69088, `https://www.gutenberg.org/files/69088/69088-h/69088-h.htm`; Econlib, `https://www.econlib.org/library/YPDBooks/Shaw/shwFS.html`; Hanover College teaching excerpt of Webb, `https://history.hanover.edu/courses/excerpts/111webb.html`.

### The seven essayists and their essays [PRIMARY — TOC, cross-checked archive.org + Econlib] — L2 attribution table

The volume is organised in three parts. **Shaw wrote two essays** (Economic + Transition) — the L2 trap of assuming one-essayist-one-essay:

|---|---|---|
| **The Basis of Socialism** | Economic | **G. Bernard Shaw** |

| | Moral | **Sydney Olivier** |
| **The Organization of Society** | Property under Socialism | **Graham Wallas** |
| | Industry under Socialism | **Annie Besant** |
| **The Transition to Social Democracy** | Transition | **G. Bernard Shaw** (his second) |
| | The Outlook | **Hubert Bland** |

Plus **Shaw's Preface** (English ed.). So: Shaw = editor + Preface + 2 essays (Economic, Transition); Webb = "Historic"; Clarke = "Industrial"; Olivier = "Moral"; Wallas = "Property under Socialism"; Besant = "Industry under Socialism"; Bland = "The Outlook." Note: Webb's essay is titled **"Historic"** under the running head "The Basis of Socialism" — hence the standard citation **"The Historic Basis of Socialism."**

### Sidney Webb's "Historic" essay — the thesis's primary [PRIMARY — verbatim, cross-checked Hanover excerpt + archive.org + search-confirmed]

*(quoted short, fair-use; the full text is public domain and consistent verbatim across the hosts above)*

- **The social organism displaces classical liberalism:** *"This new scientific conception of the social organism has put completely out of countenance the cherished principles of the political economist and the philosophic radical."*
- **The organism must grow — as axiom:** *"The necessity of the constant growth and development of the social organism has become axiomatic."*
- **Evolution as conscious co-ordination replacing competition** *(Webb quoting T. H. Huxley — attribute to Huxley, cited by Webb; NOT Webb's own coinage):* *"Evolution … is the substitution of consciously regulated co-ordination among the units of each organism, for blind anarchic competition."*
- **The engine is democracy, and the drift is one-way:** *"The main stream which has borne European society towards Socialism during the past 100 years is the irresistible progress of Democracy."* And, on the unconscious character of the drift: he insists that "unconscious socialism" has already advanced through public control of services (largely municipal) — *"our irresistible glide into collectivist Socialism."*
- **Gradualism as the only permissible mode** — the four-conditions passage (the strongest single primary for the node's "gradualness" claim): *"Important organic changes can only be (1) democratic, and thus acceptable to a majority of the people, and prepared for in the minds of all; (2) gradual, and thus causing no dislocation, however rapid may be the rate of progress; (3) not regarded as immoral by the mass of the people, and thus not subjectively demoralizing to them; and (4) in this country at any rate, constitutional and peaceful."*
- **No rupture, continuity of the social tissue:** *"No philosopher now looks for anything but the gradual evolution of the new order from the old, without breach of continuity or abrupt change of the entire social tissue at any point during the process."*
- **THE load-bearing line — the individual reduced to an administered function** (the sharpest primary evidence for society-as-administrable-organism, the-root's political form): *"The perfect and fitting development of each individual is not necessarily the utmost and highest cultivation of his own personality, but the filling, in the best possible way, of his humble function in the great social machine."*

This last line is the node's whole thesis job in Webb's own hand: the person is not an end but a *function* in a *machine*, developed toward a fit place — administrable stock guided to its station. It is the 1889 daylight cognate of `rule-by-trained-elite` and `the-root`.

## Permeation and gradualness: what is IN the 1889 volume vs. LATER (the L2 dating spine)

The node sits beside `permeation.md` and must not blur the dates. Three distinct statements, three distinct dates:

- **1889 — *Fabian Essays* (this primary):** states **evolutionary, gradual, constitutional collectivism** — Webb's four conditions and "social organism" doctrine above; Shaw's "Transition" essay argues the parliamentary/gradual road. This is gradualness **as doctrine**. It does **NOT** use the word "permeation" as a named strategy, and it contains no wire-pulling boast.
- **1892 — Shaw, *The Fabian Society: What It Has Done and How It Has Done It*, Fabian Tract No. 41** (paper read at Essex Hall, 6 Feb 1892): the explicit **permeation** boast — *"We permeated the party organizations and pulled all the wires we could lay our hands on with our utmost adroitness and energy."* This is where the *named strategy* of infiltrating existing parties is stated in the Fabians' own words. **Three years after** the Essays.
- **1923 — Sidney Webb, presidential/chairman's address to the Labour Party conference, 26 June 1923 (pub. Fabian Tract No. 207):** the motto **"the inevitability of gradualness."** **Thirty-four years after** the Essays. `permeation.md` and `rule-by-trained-elite.md` already date this correctly; do NOT let a node-builder slide it into the 1889 volume.

**Build consequence:** the node `fabian-essays` supports *"the Fabians stated gradualness/evolutionary collectivism in their own words, 1889"* at WELD. It supports the *named* "permeation" doctrine only by **`restates`/adjacency** to `permeation` (the concept), whose own primaries are the 1892 tract and the 1923 address — not this volume. Keep the volume's claim to what the volume says.

## The eugenics adjacency (R-3 split — documented, but NOT in this volume)

Several Fabians expressed eugenic / breed-the-stock views. This is `common-inheritance` / `network-exposure` in the milieu and is documented **in their other writings**, cited precisely so the 1889 volume is not smeared:

- **Shaw:** *"The only fundamental and possible Socialism is the socialization of the selective breeding of Man"* — appendix ("The Revolutionist's Handbook") to *Man and Superman*, **1903**. [documented, other work]
- **Sidney (and Beatrice) Webb:** advocacy of measures against *"race deterioration … race suicide"* and declining birth-rate among the "fit" — Sidney Webb, *The Decline in the Birth-Rate* (Fabian Tract No. 131, **1907**) and related writings. [documented, later work]
- **H. G. Wells:** eugenic passages in *Anticipations* (**1901**) and after. [documented, other work — see `wells-hg`]

**None of these is in *Fabian Essays* (1889).** The 1889 volume's argument is evolutionary-collectivist and gradualist; the eugenic register enters these authors' work later and elsewhere. Wire eugenics to the **people** nodes (or a eugenics cluster, cf. R-Q1), never to `fabian-essays` as if the volume stated it. This is the R-3 discipline: the connection (these authors later held eugenic views) is documented; the intent-import into the 1889 text is **not established** (see L3 below).
