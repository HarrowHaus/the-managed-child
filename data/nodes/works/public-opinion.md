# Public Opinion (Lippmann)

> **WELD** · map node 5.3 · 1922

The daylight statement of the administrable public: a public that cannot see the world, and so must be managed by a class that can.

## Documented core
Walter Lippmann published *Public Opinion* through **Harcourt, Brace and Company in 1922**. Its argument opens in **Chapter I, "The World Outside and the Pictures in Our Heads":** the citizen acts not on the world but on a mental image of it — **"the pictures inside the heads of these human beings… are their public opinions."** The world itself is **"too big, too complex, and too fleeting for direct acquaintance,"** so behavior **"is a response to the pseudo-environment,"** and **"for the most part we do not first see, and then define, we define first and then see."**

From that diagnosis Lippmann draws the phrase the thesis rests on. In **Chapter XV, "Leaders and the Rank and File,"** he writes: **"That the manufacture of consent is capable of great refinements no one, I think, denies. The process by which public opinions arise is certainly no less intricate than it has appeared in these pages, and the opportunities for manipulation open to anyone who understands the process are plain enough."** In the same passage: **"persuasion has become a self-conscious art and a regular organ of popular government… the practice of democracy has turned a corner. A revolution is taking place, infinitely more significant than any shifting of economic power."** The phrase *manufacture of consent* is Lippmann's own coinage, offered as description — not a hostile paraphrase, and not, as it is often assumed, Chomsky's.

The remedy is an expert class. Because the public cannot know the world, Lippmann calls for **"a specialized class whose interests reach beyond the locality"** and for **"intelligence bureaus"** — an **"independent, expert organization for making the unseen facts intelligible to those who have to make the decisions."** That is `rule-by-trained-elite` restated for the age of mass communication.

## The 1925 sharpening — kept distinct
Three years later, in ***The Phantom Public*** (Harcourt, Brace, **1925**), the diagnosis hardens: the sovereign public of democratic theory is **"a mere phantom,"** and the individual citizen **"has come to feel rather like a deaf spectator in the back row, who ought to keep his mind on the mystery off there, but cannot quite manage to keep awake."** These phrases — *deaf spectator*, *phantom*, *bewildered herd* — belong to **1925**, not to *Public Opinion*, and are dated here accordingly.

## Held-open / discard
The **Bernays tie is documented as reception, not coordination**: Bernays read Lippmann and drew on *Public Opinion* in *Crystallizing Public Opinion* (1923), and later coined the adjacent **"engineering of consent"** (1947). The chronology forbids reading Bernays as Lippmann's source — Lippmann's phrase comes first — and no shared design is claimed (R-3). Both men also sat in the **WWI federal propaganda apparatus** — Bernays in the Committee on Public Information (the "Creel Committee," 1917–19), Lippmann in Allied/CPI propaganda-intelligence work in Europe in 1918 — but co-presence in one institution documents a shared milieu, never a cabal. **Chomsky and Herman's *Manufacturing Consent* (1988)** took the phrase from Lippmann and read his advocacy as the thing they indict; that is downstream reception, and Lippmann's intent is not to be read backward through it.

## Role in the thesis
The consent rail (5.3): the root's **political face** — the administrable-public doctrine turned on the adult citizen, the sibling of the managed child. Its force is that it was **advocated in the open**, in a book still in print: the managing of the public's picture was proposed, not hidden. A symptom of the root, not a hidden cause; the kinship to the managed child is the analyst's (`convergent-selection` / `analogy`) — one root surfacing in two theaters — never a line into any child-institution.
