# James Burnham

> **WELD** · map node 3.5 · 1905-1987

The theorist who named the managerial class — and built the daylight bridge from Machiavelli's elite theory to the twentieth-century rule of managers.

## Documented core
James Burnham gave the trained-elite doctrine its twentieth-century name. In ***The Managerial Revolution: What Is Happening in the World*** (John Day, 1941) he argued that **"capitalist society will be replaced by 'managerial society'… the transition… is already well under way,"** with control shifting **"away from the capitalists proper and toward the managers"** — the administrators, bureaucrats, and technicians who run the bureaus of state and industry alike. And he read the same revolution running under three regimes at once: **"the New Deal moves in the same direction as Stalinism and Nazism,"** and Russia's upheaval was **"not a socialist revolution… but a managerial revolution."** Capitalism was ending, but socialism was not what replaced it — managerialism was.

In ***The Machiavellians: Defenders of Freedom*** (John Day, 1943) he supplied the theory beneath the diagnosis, popularizing the Italian elite-theorists — Machiavelli, Gaetano Mosca (the "ruling class" and its legitimating "political formula"), Vilfredo Pareto (the "circulation of elites"), Robert Michels (the "iron law of oligarchy") — around a single spine: **"all societies… are ruled by a minority."** This is the daylight bridge carrying the classical "a minority always rules" thesis into the managerial frame — the explicit intellectual line from the taproot to the technocrat.

## Held-open / discard
Burnham is the analytic voice, not the alarm-voice — unlike Huxley, he described the managerial revolution as inevitable and, his critic George Orwell charged, half-admired the winners. Orwell's **"Second Thoughts on James Burnham"** (*Polemic*, 1946) diagnosed exactly that **"power worship"** — "the instinct to bow down before the conqueror of the moment" — even as *Nineteen Eighty-Four* (1949) borrowed Burnham's three super-states and Goldstein's *Oligarchical Collectivism* from him: influence through critique, not discipleship. And his trajectory is documented and kept on its own dates: an ex-Trotskyist who broke with Marxism and resigned the Workers Party (21 May 1940), he **secretly joined the CIA's Office of Policy Coordination as a consultant in October 1949**, then co-founded *National Review* with Buckley (1955). The OPC fact is documented biography — and it **post-dates both books** (1941, 1943), so it is connection, not intent: the managerial thesis is published analysis, never read backward here as a covert program (R-3).

## Role in the thesis
The Administrators rail (map-node 3.5): the theorist who put a twentieth-century name to rule-by-the-fit — "the managers" — and made explicit the elite-theory inheritance the trunk had only implied. Articulation and diagnosis, not cause (S-5): no line runs from Burnham into any child-institution, and the managed child is simply one province of the managerial society he named.
