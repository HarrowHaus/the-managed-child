# Mircea Eliade

> **WELD** · map node DP.2 · 1907-1986

The scholarly-legitimacy face of the mid-century myth current — the historian of religion who made "the sacred" prestigious and exportable, through the same Eranos/Bollingen machine as Jung and Campbell.

## Documented core
Mircea Eliade built a universalizing theory of religion across four dated primaries — correcting the French-original-versus-English-translation trap along the way. *The Myth of the Eternal Return* (French 1949; English, Bollingen Series XLVI, 1954) argues that archaic humanity held nothing real unless it repeated a celestial archetype, ritual repetition abolishing profane time — its fourth chapter, "The Terror of History," casts myth as the archaic technology for defeating history's terror. *The Sacred and the Profane* (German 1957; English, Harcourt, 1959) states the core: **"sacred and profane are two modes of being in the world,"** and **"man becomes aware of the sacred because it manifests itself, shows itself, as something wholly different from the profane"** — the **hierophany**, in which "something sacred shows itself to us," experienced by the traditional *homo religiosus*. *Shamanism* (French 1951; English, Bollingen LXXVI, 1964) and *Patterns in Comparative Religion* (French 1949; English 1958) are the universalizing monograph and the systematic morphology.

The DP.2 job is the **machinery**, and it is documented at every link. Eliade lectured at Jung's **Eranos conferences** at Ascona from **1950**; a **Bollingen Foundation grant (1951)**, the Mellon patronage that also published Campbell's *Hero with a Thousand Faces*, relieved his Paris poverty and carried several works into the Bollingen Series; and in **1957** he took the University of Chicago's chair in the history of religions (to 1986), from which the "Eliadean" method became the dominant American paradigm. The tie to Campbell runs through that shared apparatus — Campbell edited *Papers from the Eranos Yearbooks* (Bollingen Series XXX) — not person to person.

## Held-open / discard
Two seams are held open, precisely. First, the **truth of "the sacred."** Scholars — Russell McCutcheon (*Manufacturing Religion*, 1997), Eliade's Chicago colleague Jonathan Z. Smith (*To Take Place*, 1987), Daniel Dubuisson, Ivan Strenski — charge that his *sui generis*, ahistorical religion is crypto-theological, not rigorous scholarship. So "the sacred" is carried here exactly as the corpus carries Campbell's monomyth: an **influential, prestige-lending template, never a proven truth about religion**.

Second, the **Iron Guard seam** (R-8). In the mid-1930s the young Eliade aligned with Romania's fascist **Legion of the Archangel Michael** under Codreanu, mentored by Nae Ionescu; he wrote for the Legionary press and in **1937** published an essay endorsing the Legion and praising Codreanu; his last pro-Legion piece appeared **February 1938**, and he kept a lifelong postwar silence about it. That **connection is documented and dated** (Volovici, Berger, Dubuisson, Lincoln, Strenski); the **degree** of complicity and whether it stains the mature scholarship is genuinely **contested** (Ricketts and Rennie hold him a sympathizer, never a formal member or a militant). The seam is kept open — not laundered into "youthful nationalism," not inflated into a fascist exposé, and not the node's center, which is the scholarship and its place in the machinery.

## Role in the thesis
The dispersal register (map-node DP.2): the scholarly-legitimacy face of the same mid-century current the corpus tracks in its pop face (Campbell) and its psychological face (Jung). Eliade is evidence of the century's appetite for a single, universal, expert-certified deep grammar of meaning — the administrable-stock disposition dignified and made exportable — a **symptom, not a cause** (S-5). No line runs from his work into any child-institution; the tie to the managed child is the analyst's, held as `analogy`, never a chain.
